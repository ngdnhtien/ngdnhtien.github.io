The first image of black holes

Newer: Màu của mây
Older: Just One Slight Smile is Very Alluring

Yesterday I had the chance to hear about the first image of Black Hole, presented by Dr Paul T. P. Ho, Director of James Clerk Maxwell telescope and Director General of East Asian Observatory, one of the seven radio observatories took part in the historic mission: to capture the first image of a black hole.

There wasn’t much unrevealed stuff about the project as I expected. With a gifted sense of humour, Dr Paul however did well in describing us the simple, elegant method that was used by the Event Horizon Telescope’s operator. He also brought great insights into some particularly interesting properties of Masier 87. A few crucial points I think worth-noticing:

  1. Masier 87 was chosen not only because of its size, but also its angular velocity. Although Sagittarius A* does have a closer distance to the Earth, which theoretically easier to be observed, it is 1000 times smaller than Masier 87. In addition to this, Masier 87 has a much longer cycle due to its lower angular velocity compared to Sagittarius A*’s one; thus, it allows us to receive data accurately since the Doppler effect plays a huge role in obstructing the data that is being sent to our system on Earth.

  2. Event Horizon Telescope is collecting, analyzing and synthesizing data from both Masier 87 and Sagittarius A* simultaneously. For the Masier 87’s case, the team is trying to improve the array’s resolution by adding more observers all around the world and taking pictures from different wave-length. For the Sagittarius A* case, surprisingly, the team is looking forward to publishing its picture within this year.

  3. Next-generation instruments will have more resolution and more sensitivity (time domain, energy domain, dynamics domain).
